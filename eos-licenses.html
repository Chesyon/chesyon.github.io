First things first: IANAL. I've done some research, but none of this is legal advice. If I've made a mistake, please politely let me know.

I'm an Explorers of Sky ROMhacker. Most of the libraries used for modifying Explorers of Sky (namely, skytemple-files and ndspy) are licensed under GPLv3. This means that if I use any of their code in my own tools/patches (this includes just using them as an import), I am legally required to release my own tool/patch under GPLv3. I personally find this annoying, but I'll spare my angry ramblings for a footnote.

I personally do not care what people do with my code, so long as I receive credit for my work. I do this as a hobby, I don't care about money. It was very difficult to find clear information on this, but to my knowledge, I am allowed to license my source code separately.

That being said, for my Explorers of Sky hacking tools/patches, I have chosen to license my source code under the MIT license. What this means is that while the distribution itself is under GPLv3 (by requirement), you may use my code under MIT, as long as you also follow the licenses of any used libraries. This means that if you were to remove any usage of GPLv3 dependencies from my code (which, admittedly, is easier said than done), you may do whatever you please with it without being bound by GPLv3.

For my ASM patches in particular, only the patch handlers (the patch.py files) use GPLv3. This does mean that the skypatch as a whole is distributed under GPLv3, but the individual ASM files do not have that restriction. You can redistribute that code freely under the MIT license, such as by applying them in a ROMhack. The skypatch file is ultimately just a tool for applying the ASM code, so just because you use one of my skypatches does not mean you suddenly have to license your entire hack under GPLv3. That probably wouldn't even be possible. Perhaps all of that was obvious, but I couldn't find clear answers on any of this for a while, so I'd like to save other people that digging.
